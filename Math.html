Math Modeling

Mathematical Modeling with Mr. Regele was my first introduction to the world of competitive math as well as to the world of math modeling. Before I came to MAMS, I had never partaken in a mathematics competition or done any sort of creative problem solving. That all changed and I quickly learned the scope of both the applications of mathematics and the extent to which competitive mathematics can be taken. Throughout the year we consistently did assignments that encouraged us to build upon prior knowledge and find out ourselves what would often be given simply as a formula or a rule to memorize. This allowed me to understand math in a way that I never had before, allowing me to come up with my own unique solutions to problems. We also learned from Mr. Regele that while sometimes asking, "Why should I learn this if I am not going to use this in the real world?" may be useful, it is often okay to answer, "Because it's fun."

The first assignment that you will find here is my teams HiMC paper. HiMC stands for High School Mathematical Contest in Modeling and is an annual two weeklong competition open to the entire United States, which allows students to join a group and write a paper as a response to a mathematical modeling prompt. However at MAMS our experience was a little bit different, as we had only 48 hours to work on our paper. As a result, while the experience was a bit hectic, many of the students agree that it is one of the most memorable experiences of the year. It allowed us all to learn something new and to tackle a very open-ended large-scale problem in our own way, building our own timeframe and making our own decisions. My group, which consisted of Smita Bhogle, Isabella Doyle, Rachel Bunsick, and I, decided to tackle Problem B, which focused on simulating the water level of Lake Meade in order to find out if the level would one day become critically low. This is important as a large portion of the South-West, mainly parts of Arizona and Nevada, depend on the artificial lake as a water source. We chose this problem as when we had been practicing earlier working on modeling problems the week before, we ended up finding out that statistical analysis problems were more easily understandable to our team, and out of the two problems available that year, Problem B was based more on statistics than Problem A (both problems can be found at the link for the HiMC website near the beginning of this paragraph). In essentiality, in order to answer whether or not the water level of Lake Meade would become a problem in the future, we ended up creating a simulation that contained functions based off of past data to simulate what the water levels would look like in the future. By finding climate data for the region, as well as Colorado flow data on the Climate Data Online website, we were able to use theoretical inflow and outflow equations, as well as temperature data to model how the elevation of the lake would change over time. We were able to code a java program that used those equations that allowed us to conclude that if everything continued as it had been for the past 30 years, the water level would reach unsafe levels within the next 20 years. At the end of this experience, we wrote a paper on our solution to the problem, which was then submitted to the COMAP commission for review.

Hanford Part #1

The second assignment that you are seeing here was given to us in late B-term which required us to find the least-square line of best fit for a set of data without using any pre-coded software features. This was a great learning experience as instead of simply accepting that a tool exists, we were able to pick apart the process by which the least-square line of best fit is generated, and using this knowledge, were able to apply this information to a set of data. After learning how to perform the calculations, we were tasked with creating a writeup of our solution in Mathematica in a way that was understandable, organized, and complete. This is what you can find to the left of this text. As with many Mathematica assignments in Mr. Regele's class, there was a large focus on visual data representation, which is why you will find many headings, sub-headings, graphs, and tables in the write-up.

Epsilon School

This particular assignment was a group project. The goal of this project was to model the optimal departments for which to hire faculty, following an increase in student enrollment. This was one of the first experiences in the class of a problem that was not just multi-stepped, but rather something more open ended. As it was so open ended, we ended up having many different approaches in the class. It was interesting to listen to the presentations and find out how everyone approached the problem. In my group we decided on figuring out the population of the following year, and then scaling class enrollments for the next year using the previous years grade by grade class enrollment ratios. This then allowed us to find the classes that would have the highest student to teacher ratios the following year, and teachers were then assigned to those classes.
