STEM

STEM1 with Dr. Crowthers is a course at MAMS that allowed me to continue my track record of completing an independent research project every year of high school. Over the course of this 5 month long project we were given time to complete research on a subject of our own interest, come up with a plan for testing, complete that testing, analyze our results, and conclude the project with a school wide science fair. When I first began this year, I knew that I wanted to do research that focused on both thermal energy storage and on energy generation. Those two topics went well together, but it was not until Bournedale when I had an idea of leveraging and storing computer heat. After doing much research over the course of A-Term and B-Term, I came up with my final idea of storing computer heat using a phase change material, and then utilizing that heat by converting it to electricity through thermoelectric generation. If you want to learn more about my project, you can scroll down and find out what it was all about, or you can alternatively read the documents found in the linked folder (LINKKKKKKK). Enhancing Computer Heat Sink Design with Phase Change Thermal Energy Storage and Thermoelectric Generators

Overview

SAMPLE TEXT

A problem that plagues modern computing is that computer cooling solutions are often insufficient in their cooling abilities and the heat drawn from computer processors is underutilized and discarded into the environment. It is important to cool a processor in order reduce the chance of circuit failure and to upkeep the performance of the processor. Phase change materials can cool a substance by storing heat as molecular potential energy. As such, the engineering objective is to design a computer heatsink that can effectively cool a central processing unit through utilization of a phase change material, and simultaneously generate electricity via thermoelectric generation. To do this a device was built that used a paraffin wax as a phase change material to store heat, and thermoelectric generators for power generation. To determine design effectiveness, temperature and power production data were measured when the design was used as the sole cooling system. With this design, the temperature of the CPU exceeded 85 degrees Celsius, which is unsafe for long term operation. However, when a conductive copper structure was introduced, the maximum CPU temperature decreased by more than 5 degrees Celsius to within acceptable temperatures. The power produced by the generators was low, reaching a peak of 14mW. The maximum heat to electricity conversion efficiency was only 0.03%, and thus improvements in thermoelectric generation technology may be necessary. Even this small power output can be used for low power tasks, and this energy will be generated even after the computer has been powered off. This research can be used to develop one of the first combined heat storage and utilization systems for computer applications. Future research may want to focus on experimenting with synthetic phase change materials and on optimization of the thermoelectric generators.
