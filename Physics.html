Physics

Physics with Mr. Ellis is a class that allows me and the other students to learn about the fundamental basics of kinematics, energy, momentum, forces, rotational motion, electricity and magnetism, as well as many other subjects. Every day was spent learning something new, and every problem that we solved felt unique and provided some new knowledge for us to further our conceptual understanding with. The never-ending challenge helped teach us to think critically about physics, as well as encouraging us to aspire to be creative in the often-analytical subject of physics. As a result of the knowledge that I have learned in physics, I now have a better understanding of the world that we live in. We began learning each subject through notes, and then moved into End of Chapter/EOC questions. These were more difficult but were more similar to the questions that we would find on the tests, so this allowed us to apply or knowledge of the subjects thus far so that we could more readily solve problems. After this there was usually a project or a LAB, and we then prepared the exams with some sample tests from previous years. We then had our final exam for every unit, and then it was onto the next unit! There was plenty of fun along the way, including but not limited to a variety of candy to pick from in class, as well as races to get to class before class started and the door was closed!

Über-Rocket

The first example of the work that we did in physics that I have here is the Über-rocket problem. In many of the units that we have done, there is an Über problem. While not always the most challenging, they teach prudence as they usually contain many steps of calculations, so a single mistake can change the final result drastically. This particular example is the algebra version of our first Über problem, which involved finding the total displacement of an object through the use of multi-stage kinematics. In order to solve this problem I broke up the total calculation into many different sections, with the object acting differently in each. I was then able to complete the calculations for the first section, use those results to move onto the calculations for the second sections, etc., until I got my final answer. Then after this we were tasked with creating a double page write-up of our solution. This particular write-up allowed us to show how well we can concisely fit all of our work into a single double-page file while still containing all of the necessary information.

Penny Drop Lab

The second example of my work is the lab-report from our first ever lab where we estimated the acceleration due to gravity on earth by timing the fall of a penny. In order to calculate the acceleration due to gravity, we were given some measuring materials and a penny. We then tried different setups, until we settled on a final one. After this we collected data about the time it took the penny to hit the ground from different heights. Using this data we were able to leverage the kinematic equations to calculate the acceleration due to gravity using both raw and linearized data. While my groups results were not necessarily the most accurate, this lab did teach our entire grade a valuable lesson, which is that there are always certain limitations to every experimental setup, and often times, human error is a large factor that should be minimized to the greatest extent possible. In addition to the data collection we also practiced being concise by creating a single page lab report, thus having to fit all of the information onto a small space.
