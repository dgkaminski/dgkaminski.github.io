Humanities

In Humanities with Mrs. Small, I was able to have my first experience of a combined history and English class. One aspect of the class that I enjoyed most was the discussions, which allowed myself and my peers to share our ideas about topics found in assigned readings, as well as anything else that we found interesting. The fact that these discussions were not completely rigid allowed for a more varied and interesting experience during class. Aside from the discussions and readings, much of the time in class was spent working on essays, and constantly improving our writing styles and ability to express ourselves. Two examples of these essays can be found below. We also had some more creative assignments. One of these was a skit about the book Walden by Henry David Thoreau, and we all were able to present these during Bournedale. This was a super fun time and I loved seeing some humorous skits and amazing acting from my peers. We also had some time to work on a satire piece with a group. This could be in the form of a video, new article, letter, or almost anything else, and each and every one of them turned out to be very humorous.

Ray Bradbury Essay

The first essay that I wrote focused on exploring the pieces, “August 2026: There Will Come Soft Rains” and “The Pedestrian” by Ray Bradbury. In these pieces Ray Bradbury criticizes the use of technology as an end-all be-all solution to many of the world’s problems and points out that no matter how advanced technology may get, it will never replicate true humanity. This is what I analyzed in this essay, and I ended up pointing out that while technology may initially seem to be helping humanity progress in a new direction, it may be used for purposes that were not originally intended, thus harming humanity in the long run. In this way, the progress of technology may end up being regress for humanity.

Satirical Analysis Essay

The second essay shown here is an analysis of a satirical piece titled, "Scots Vote to Stay in UK; ‘Yes’ Voters to Be Hanged as Rebels." While we were given many options on how we could analyze the satire of our choosing, I chose to analyze how and why it failed in achieving its end goal. With this specific satire, I ended up agreeing that while there were some parts of the piece that were able to convey the message of the satire, overall the piece was still lacking the necessary information and writing style that would allow it to truly convince the reader to believe in their cause. In this case the author was ultimately unable to convey that Britain has been suppressing Scottish independence movements due to their overuse of sharp language and misuse of caricature.
